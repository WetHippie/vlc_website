Snowy Mountains 2002 - Day 3 and 4


Sunrise in Jindabyne looking over the mountains
Lake Jindabyne in early morning sunshine

At the start of Day 3, things were rather laid back. A gorgeous mountain sunrise had me out taking photos. Wandering back at about 0700 for a bit of brekkie and a chat, it seems a number of us hadn't registered yet, and I needed to get a T-shirt so we headed down to the signup area. After enquiring about the shirts, there were none left but there was a chance there were still some left in Cooma. With card in hand, I decided to race off there to get some, telling the others that I would meet them back at Jindabyne (half hadn't even woken up at this point).


Carpark and the outside of Mad Mooses

A brisk ride in the cool morning air was refreshing and soon found myself at Cooma. With a couple of T-shirts in hand, I meander back to the bike. As I'm doing the jacket up, I get biffed over the head. Turning around I see the helmeted face of a mate of mine Mick The Merciless Quigley from back over on the WAR list when I lived in Perth. He'd warned me he'd be here and it was good to catch up again. He's now riding a YZF1000 with pillion. After the usual bit of gasbagging, he tells me to have a look at the visitor centre. There's a done-up beemer over there. With camera in hand, I wander over to look at it. The darn thing has a VW engine fitted into the frame. A very nice job too as it looks like there didn't need to be any modifications done to the frame at all.


Various shots of the beemer with the VW engine fitted.
The approach road - down the hill and around the one lane bridge

After this, Mick and I decide to head back via Dalgety. There's a fun ride from there back to meeting the others at Cooma, plus we get the last stamp for the ride raffle at the end of the day (you only need 3 to enter). Pulling into the carpark we gain the required stamps and turn around to see a fleet of VFRs entering. Ah, the boys have woken up obviously. Gordon is there and how has his own bike - one slightly damaged 02 VFR. Since Graeme couldn't ride at all, and Gordy's without, a problem was solved. We wander over to show Mick the works of wonder achieved the night before and then chat about what's happening next. The others are heading to Cooma to get a stamp, then head back to Jindabyne to submit the cards and then we'll head off somewhere for the day.


The road on the way in - sweepers that are tighter than they look

Wandering off up the road back to Jindabyne, we were given a warning by the others that some cattle were being driven up the road. No sooner were we on the road when I slowed right down. Cow pats everywhere and not a cow to be seen. Cautiously heading up the road,we of course encounter the cattle at the best part of the road - a series of tight corners with long straights between on a steeeep uphill section. Ah, bugger. :( Oh well, lots of nice pictures and it was a struggle trying to not scare the calves with the exhausts of the bike. I would have hated to startle one (they were all over the road and you had to ride between them!) and have it run into the bike. Even as littlies they way a couple of hundred kilos and you won't have a chance of holding the bike up if they hit you.


Drover and dogs herding a straggler up the hill
Read the stickers. Huge photo so you get the details

Handing my ticket in at Cooma, I settle in to have a look around at the various bikes that were there. You can do whatever you want during the day, so long as you get the three stamps required. There's short runs like what we just did, or you can do the full loop like we did the day before. Mick organises a test ride for a Valkyrie and ST13000 while I hang around taking pictures and waiting for the rest to turn up. An hour goes by and no sign of them. Wonder what they're up to? - too many coffees in Cooma? Oh well, organise a ride on the Blackbird test bike and off I go. By this time, it's a couple of hours later and I'm really starting to get pissed at the guys. No messages on the mobile, nothing. Mick's bought a ticket for lunch at the top of the Thredbo chairlift so that sounds like a plan. Stuff 'em.

A quick jaunt out to Thredbo then takes place. 30km of nice sweepers taken for the second time in two days, so it's much faster and less scary this time. Pull up in the carpark and go searching for the chairlift as the lunch ticket is for the restaurant at the end of the chairlift. This is a great ride and quite a long one too. The scenery is spectacular as you head up the mountain. There's still a reasonable amount of snow around for this time of the year and the temperature - it's hovering around 25-27 during the day. Surprisingly none of the snow looks like it it is melting.


The view up the chairlift to where I'm having lunch

Some photos taken from the top of the mountain. From left to right is the entrance road into Thredbo. The brown line through the trees is where the road goes. Next is some ugly guy posing for a photo to prove he really is there. Finally a view down the chairlift looking at the Thredbo ski village itself.


Highly unoriginal advertising on the chairlift

I'd arrived a little early to the restaurant so I tucked into some food before the Mick showed up. Sat there admiring the scenery. Some time about now an SMS turns up from Chris "hi, we've gone to the CAVEs". Prick. Great timing. Only about 3 hours too late! After calming down and firing off a "thanks a lot you bastard" message I tucked into a yummy Real Aussie burger and chips, then Mick shows up. They eaty lunch, we take a few photos and then headed back down the mountain. The tickets also gave us some free rides on the bobsled at the bottom of the mountain so we took that on too before needing to head back to Jindabyne for Mick's test ride appointments (at an abnormally slow pace for me, content just to sit behind Mick and follow his lines).

Arriving back at Jindabyne at about 1540 we find a carpark chockas with bikes (as you can see on the right. As usual at these sorts of gatherings, there are all sorts of bikes here. Everything from trikes to cruisers to sportsbikes. Lots of L-platers, which is great to see. And, one thing that we noticed during the weekend - the huge numbers of VFRs that have attended. As a rough guess, probably one in seven bikes was a VFR.

While wandering the crowd I come across a very nicely put together Vtr. It's all yellow, with a lower fairing done in silver. High-mount pipes and other go-fast bits make it quite a neat looking package, so I strike up a conversation with the owner, who just happens to be leaning on the bike at the time. Usual stuff about what's been done to the respective bikes and what other bikes we have and such and I mention that I'm trying to sell my VFR400 race bike. Seems he has an interest in acquiring same. So after a few more minutes of chatting I hand him the contact details and hopefully I've got at least someone interested in it.


The carpark at the finish area. There's about 300 or so bikes here currently

After a while, I meander back up to the lodge to see what is going on. Over time the other riders filter in. Finally it's time to go down to the presentation, but we have to work out some way of getting Graeme down there. With his stuffed knee and two walking sticks, he's not going anywhere fast. As usual, the beer starts talking - mmm.... trolley.... that could be useful. Graeme, come here! And, as you see, it's a complete success. We "strap" him to the trolley with a couple of ocky straps, stick his lid on him, just in case he falls and off we go down the hill. There were some mighty confused looking people and cage drivers looking at us, but we were ultimately successful in managing to get him down to the presentation area.

Pre-race preparations. Trying to psych out the opposition.
Gentlemen, start you engines
Wheelie Master Extraordinaire!

It seems to be rather traditional that the presentation time is going to be cold and windy. The sun is on its way down and the seabreeze has made its way inland from the coast. The altitude doesn't help at all either, so it gets really cold. Fortunately, the guy doing most of the talking is a real barrell of laughs. He takes the crap out of everyone and everything, and is really quite good - but I doubt he'd ever get a professional job doing it - jokes are taken at everyone's expense, including the sponsors, but it is really quite funny. Unfortunately, none of our group won any prizes, so we left empty handed. The presentation is over an hour in length and the cold weather is effecting my bod quite a bit. As soon as the last bit is over I bolt back to the relatively close lodge to take a leak and hope everyone else will make it back with Graeme easily.

Relief over, I wander upstairs to the comfy lounges and kick back to wait for the rest to arrive. A few souls wander in like Ross and Julie (that's two of them in this group!) and Chris. They're taking their time though, so I just continue to lie on the lounge with the eyes shut. Suddenly there's a loud noise and lots of clattering and banging coming up the stairs. Opening my eyes I'm greated to the sight of the trolley that once contained Graeme to now be stacked to the top with cartons of beer and frozen pizzas. Pity I didn't have a camera handy because it would have made a great shot. By my recollection, there were three cases and 12 pizzas strapped to the trolley for the trip. Ah, dinner has arrived.

While the oven is being kicked into gear, the beer is handed out and several of them attempt to kick the DVD player into life so that we could watch Lord of the Rings. Appliance skills do not seem to be in their resume. I'm too pissed off with them to be bothered getting up and helping them, but it's pretty obvious that a cable wasn't connected up because every time they set the audio on, nothing was coming out. Sure enough, an hour or so later, the owners come back and turn around and waddayknow, the audio cables are unplugged. A couple of pieces of pizza later and I'm feeling a bit tired, so I wander off to bed for the night.

Bye Bye bike.

Waking at my usual crack-of-dawn time I shower, have a quick breakfast and then jump on the bike to head home. I'm not really interested in riding with the rest so I just pack the bags and go. As I leave at about 0730, it's still quite early, so I elect to just head straight home rather than heading up the twisty Snowy Mountains Highway. There would be a lot of dew on the road and if I do head that way, I know I wouldn't be able to control myself and probably end up in the bush due to the slippery pavement. After this, the trip home is relatively boring. Just highway cruising, sitting on 130 all the way home. I'm home by eleven, unpack, turn on the cricket, lie back in the lounge and promptly fall asleep for the next three hours - the preceding days having caught up with me finally. Next morning, I'm back on the plane to the US.

In summary, the bike handled really well once I'd completely sorted the suspension out. However, I've come to the conclusion that I really do not like the M1s at all. There is zero feel about what the front of the bike is doing - you can't tell what the road surface is like, whether the wheel is gripping or sliding and a lot of other really important details you need to know when riding hard. That, combined with the fact I seem to be able to effortlessly spin the rear up (albeit much more controllably than the 207s I once ran on there), really have me convinced that they aren't a good road tyre. I'm running 208GPs on the RSVR in seattle and they're nice, but I think I'll stick with the Bridgestones from now on. It's a pity to have to sell the Vtr as it's a great bike to ride. I really would keep it if it was worth my while, but with the intention to live in the US for a number of years, and that it would be close to impossible to get it registered here with the number of mods I've done to it, means it has to go. Sad to see this one go really. I'll miss it greatly.
